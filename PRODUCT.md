# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Internal auditors and procurement committee members in Thai government agencies who review TOR (Terms of Reference) drafts submitted by other departments. They use the tool during procurement review cycles to cross-check compliance, not to draft fixes themselves. They arrive with domain expertise in Thai procurement law but limited time and high accountability — a missed violation creates legal liability.

Secondary context: academic and demo audiences (course instructors, policy researchers) who need to understand the tool's output without deep procurement domain knowledge.

## Product Purpose

TORDIT audits completed TOR documents for compliance with Thai procurement regulations (ว 159, ว 214, ระเบียบ 2560). A user uploads a PDF, selects the procurement type and form variant, and receives a structured report: which clauses are non-compliant, which rules they violate, and what the corrected text should say. Every finding carries a rule ID and citation — the credibility of the tool rests entirely on the accuracy and verifiability of those references.

Success means an auditor can open the report, see a ranked list of violations with exact citations, and bring that to a committee review without needing to re-verify each finding manually.

## Brand Personality

Clear, reliable, calm. A professional instrument that reduces review anxiety rather than adding to it. Trustworthy because every claim is cited and every citation is real. Not intimidating — approachable enough for a first-time user, structured enough for an expert.

Reference feel: SCB/KBank mobile banking apps — Thai-first typography, clean information hierarchy, professional without being sterile or bureaucratic.

## Anti-references

- Thai government portals (e-GP, กรมบัญชีกลาง): cluttered data tables, dated typography, institutional blue header bars, overly formal visual hierarchy.
- Generic AI SaaS (2024–2026 wave): cream/sand backgrounds, hero metrics with large numbers, gradient text, identical icon-card grids, "Supercharge your workflow" voice.
- Legal compliance software (LexisNexis-style): heavy navy palette, gold accents, serif-heavy typography, formal to the point of intimidation.
- Consumer/B2C apps: playful rounded shapes, bright multi-color palettes, onboarding mascots — mismatched to a professional accountability context.

## Design Principles

1. **Findings earn their credibility.** Every violation claim must visually surface its citation and rule ID. Information without provenance is noise. Design that buries the citation undermines the tool's core promise.
2. **Reduce review anxiety.** A committee auditor approaching a 20-finding report should feel the information is manageable. Hierarchy, grouping by TOR section, and clear severity signals matter more than visual decoration.
3. **Thai first.** The UI is in Thai. Typography choices must render Thai script at reading sizes with adequate line spacing and weight contrast — decisions that work for Latin text don't automatically carry over.
4. **Professional, not institutional.** Trusted the way a Thai banking app is trusted — clean, precise, modern — not authoritative the way a government portal is authoritative (which often reads as inaccessible).
5. **Precision over polish.** Visual elements that clarify the audit report stay; elements that merely decorate are removed. The information hierarchy is the design.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

No hard WCAG level mandated. In practice: sufficient contrast for text-heavy compliance content read in office lighting, keyboard navigability for the findings panel, and Thai text at body size (14–16px) with adequate line-height (1.6–1.75) for extended reading. Reduced-motion preference respected for any transitions.
